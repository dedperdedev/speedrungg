# Anatomy of a Meaningful Choice

Greg Costikyan, "I Have No Words and I Must Design" (1994/2002). Old but the most rigorous decomposition of "meaningful choice" — the soul of game design.

## What makes a choice meaningful

A choice is meaningful if it has:

1. **Awareness** — player understands the choice exists
2. **Information** — player has enough info to evaluate options (or accepts the unknown)
3. **Consequence** — outcomes differ measurably
4. **Reminder** — the consequences echo later (you remember the choice)

Missing any → the "choice" is fake.

## Six types of choice (Costikyan)

### 1. Decisions
- Strategic, with consequences over time
- Example: skill tree allocation

### 2. Tactics
- Moment-to-moment under time pressure
- Example: combat positioning

### 3. Negotiations
- Choices within social framework
- Example: trade, alliance, faction commitment

### 4. Discoveries
- Choices that reveal info, not committing yet
- Example: probe a new area, peek at hidden card

### 5. Diplomacy
- Choices interacting with players' choices
- Example: cooperate or betray

### 6. Risk Assessments
- Choosing under known probability
- Example: high-risk-high-reward fork

## Operational test

For any "choice" you offer, answer:

- What does the player gain from option A vs B?
- Would different player types pick differently? Why?
- Will the player remember this choice 10 minutes later? An hour? A session later?
- Is there one obviously dominant option? (If yes, it's not a choice — it's a tax.)

## Common failure modes

### False choice
"Pick a starting weapon" but they're DPS-equivalent → choice is cosmetic, not meaningful.

### Unknowable choice
"Pick faction A or B" with zero info on what they do → guesses, not choice.

### Reversible choice (risk-free)
Save-scumming makes every choice meaningless. Either prevent it or design around it.

### Forced "choice"
Quest gives 3 options but all lead to identical outcome → wastes player attention.

### Decision fatigue
20 character builders before gameplay → players bounce. Front-load fewer, deeper choices; unlock more over time.

## Web-game implications

- Browser sessions are short → meaningful choices need fast consequences
- New-player onboarding has minutes, not hours → first meaningful choice in first 60s
- Mobile sessions especially → simplify decision space, deepen consequence
