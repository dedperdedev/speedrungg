# Jobs To Be Done — For Games

Christensen's framework. Adapted for games: people don't buy games for features. They "hire" games to do a job in their life.

## The frame

> When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome].

Examples:

- When I'm winding down before bed, I want to play something low-pressure, so I can relax without screen-stress.
- When I'm waiting in line, I want a quick distraction, so I can pass time without committing.
- When I'm with my kids, I want something we can play together, so I can connect without competing.
- When I'm stressed at work, I want to feel competent at something, so I can reset my self-image.

## Why JTBD beats demographics

Demographics: "Casual gamers, 25–40, mobile-first."
JTBD: "People who want to feel productive in 5-minute breaks."

The second is actionable: it tells you what the game must do, not who's allowed to play.

## Discovering the jobs

Through interviews:

- Tell me about the last time you played a game like this.
- Where were you?
- How did you feel before? After?
- What were you avoiding by playing?
- What were you trying to accomplish?

Don't ask "what features do you want?" People don't think in features.

## Multiple jobs per person

The same person hires different games for different jobs:

- Slay the Spire on commute (focus, mastery)
- Stardew Valley before bed (decompression)
- Apex Legends with friends Saturday (social bonding)

If your game tries to do all three, it does none. Pick a job.

## The competition is anything that does the job

Your competitor isn't only "other puzzle games." It's:

- Twitter (also passes 5 minutes)
- Reading
- Phone calls
- Sudoku in newspaper

Players have limited attention. You're competing for it against everything.

## Defining the job your game does

Write it down. Honestly.

Bad: "Our game is a casual platformer for fans of pixel art."
Good: "When commuters want to pass 10 minutes without thinking too hard, our game is a mindless-but-charming platformer they can pick up and put down."

The second names the situation, the motivation, the alternative. That's your real positioning.

## Finding gaps

For your game's job, who else is competing? What jobs are UNDER-served?

Example: "When parents want a wholesome, calming game to play with kids age 5-8 that doesn't require literacy."

How many existing games serve this? Maybe 2-3. There's a gap. That's your opportunity.

## Anti-patterns

### Multiple jobs masquerading as one
"Our game is for casual + competitive players." Pick one. Try to do both, do neither well.

### Job too narrow
"For 30-year-olds with red hair who like Wednesday morning chess." Real jobs are broader.

### Job too broad
"For everyone who likes fun." Useless.

### Demographics dressed up as JTBD
"For the 25-34 mobile-first segment seeking lifestyle entertainment." That's a demographic. Where's the situation?

## How to use in design

For every feature:

- Does this serve the chosen job?
- Does it conflict with the job (e.g., adding leaderboards to a relaxation game)?
- Is the job clear in the first 30 seconds of play (does the game advertise its job correctly)?

If a feature doesn't serve the job, cut it.
