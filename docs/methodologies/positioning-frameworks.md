# Positioning Frameworks — For Indie Games

Adapted from Trout, Lochhead, others. How to make your game findable in a sea of games.

## The promise of positioning

Positioning is what your audience says about you when you're not in the room.

If they say "it's like X but Y" — you have positioning.
If they say "uh, I think it's a game?" — you don't.

## The Trout/Ries positioning statement

```
For [target audience]
who [unmet need / situation],
[product name] is the [category]
that [unique value/twist].
Unlike [primary competitor],
we [key differentiator].
```

Example (Stardew Valley, retroactively):

```
For RPG fans who like Harvest Moon-style farming,
who feel modern farming sims are too gamey or shallow,
Stardew Valley is the cozy farming RPG
that combines deep simulation with rich character relationships.
Unlike Harvest Moon, we offer modding, multiplayer, and adult themes.
```

Each line MUST be specific. "RPG fans" is okay. "Gamers" is not.

## Lochhead's "Category design"

Don't position INSIDE a category. Position by NAMING a new category.

- "Roguelike deckbuilder" was a Slay the Spire-creating category
- "Vampire Survivors-likes" became a category
- "Walking simulator" started as derogatory, became a category
- "Cozy game" wasn't a category until ~2020

If your game can be BOTH the first and best in a new category, that's gold.

## Blue Ocean adaptation

Look at the dimensions all competitors compete on:

- Graphics
- Length
- Difficulty
- Multiplayer
- Setting

Most competitors crowd middle. Find dimensions to:

- ELIMINATE: which assumed-essential are unnecessary?
- REDUCE: which can be much smaller?
- RAISE: which can be much higher?
- CREATE: which doesn't exist in this market yet?

Examples:

- Vampire Survivors ELIMINATED the visual fidelity expectation, RAISED density of action, CREATED the survivor-likes category.
- Stardew Valley RAISED the depth and modding, REDUCED the multiplayer focus, CREATED the cozy-farming RPG.

## The 7-word pitch test

If you can't describe your game in ≤ 7 words that distinguish it, you don't have positioning yet.

- "Roguelike deckbuilder" — 2 words, clear
- "Cozy fishing game with social mechanics" — 6 words, clear
- "An RPG with a unique twist where you fight monsters and explore dungeons and craft items and..." — no positioning

## Where positioning lives

The positioning statement isn't customer-facing. It's the source-of-truth document. From it derives:

- Store page header
- Trailer first 5 seconds
- Tagline
- Tweet announcing
- One-paragraph description on stores
- Conversation with press

All of those should ladder back to the positioning. If your trailer says one thing and your Steam page says another, you have positioning fragmentation.

## Anti-patterns

### Positioning by genre alone
"It's a platformer." So are 5,000 others.

### Positioning by feature
"It has a crafting system!" Most people don't care about your features in the abstract.

### Positioning by vibe alone
"Cozy, atmospheric, narrative-driven." Says nothing concrete.

### Positioning that mirrors competitors
"Like Hollow Knight but..." If your only differentiator is "but," you're a derivative.

### Positioning that's untrue
"Most accessible roguelike" — be ready to defend it. If you're not actually most-accessible, you'll lose trust on first review.

## 2025–2026 marketing reality check

The marketing channels landscape shifted significantly in 2025. Some honest updates:

### TikTok organic for indie games — collapsed in 2025
Same content that hit 800K views in 2024 now averages 300–800 views. Causes:
- Algorithmic deprioritization of meme-format devlog humor
- US regulatory uncertainty
- Recommended ad budgets ($500–$2,000) above most solo budgets
- Wishlist conversion <1% even on viral hits

**Implication:** Don't plan marketing strategy around TikTok organic. If you do TikTok, treat it as bonus, not foundation. Verify with current data before investing time.

### What still works (early 2026)
- **YouTube long-form devlogs** — slower to grow but durable; subscriber-loyal audience
- **Reddit** (r/IndieDev, r/IndieGaming, genre-specific subs) — direct community engagement; avoid spammy posting
- **Discord** — your own server + cross-promotion with dev friends
- **Direct email list** — even small (100-1000) drives high-conversion launches
- **Press / coverage** — niche outlets matter more than mainstream
- **Steam Page wishlists** — still primary indicator for Steam launches

### What to be cautious about
- **Steam Next Fest** — higher-risk in 2025–2026 (AI-art accusations, review-bombing campaigns documented)
- **Crowdfunding** — Kickstarter for games has cooled; most indie games don't hit funding goals
- **Web3/blockchain games** — toxic association with casual web-game audience as of 2026; no resurgence
