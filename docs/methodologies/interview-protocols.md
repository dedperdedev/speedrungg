# Interview Protocols — Open-Ended Questions

How to interview audiences, playtesters, and the project owner without leading them.

## The asymmetric rule

The interviewer's role is **listening**. Aim for 80% them, 20% you.

If you're talking more, you're not interviewing.

## Question types — best to worst

### Best: Open recall
"Tell me about the last time you played a game like this."

Forces the participant to retrieve memory, not invent answers.

### Good: Hypothetical with grounding
"If you saw this on Steam, what would you click on first?"

Hypothetical but tied to specific context they know.

### Okay: Comparison
"How does this feel compared to [game they named earlier]?"

Comparisons are useful but anchor on the comparison, not the absolute.

### Risky: Yes/no
"Did you like it?"

Useless without a follow-up. Always pair: "Why?"

### Worst: Leading
"You found that intuitive, right?"

Confirms bias. Refuse this question type even when asking yourself.

## The follow-up pattern

After every answer, before moving to next planned question:

- "Tell me more about that"
- "Why?"
- "What did you mean by [their specific word]?"
- "When was the last time you felt that?"

3-5 follow-ups per planned question. Real depth comes from follow-ups, not from many planned questions.

## The 5 whys

When you hit something interesting, drill:

```
Q: Why did you stop playing?
A: It got boring.

Q: What was boring about it?
A: Same enemies over and over.

Q: When did it start feeling repetitive?
A: After like 20 minutes.

Q: What did you do to push through?
A: I didn't, I just stopped.

Q: If you could change one thing, what?
A: More variety.
```

Now you have a specific finding (variety after 20 minutes), not a vague "boring."

## What NOT to do

### Defend the game
Participant: "I didn't get the controls."
You: "Oh, that's because most people use WASD..."

NO. They told you the truth: controls don't communicate. Don't argue. Take notes.

### Lead with conclusions
"So I think the issue is the difficulty. What do you think?"

You've poisoned the answer. They'll agree.

### Move on too fast
After a juicy answer, the temptation is "great, next question." Resist. Drill 3-5 follow-ups before moving on.

### Make them feel judged
"Most people figure that out quickly. Why didn't you?"

They'll stop being honest. Treat every "mistake" as design feedback.

### Combine multiple questions
"Was it fun and what would you change and how often would you play?"

Pick one. Address it. Move to next.

## Recording

Always record (with permission). Notes during interview = missed details. Listen back, transcribe key moments.

For online: Zoom record + transcript export. For in-person: phone mic.

## Saturation rule

If 3 consecutive interviews say the same thing about a topic, you have saturation on that topic. Move on.

If 3 consecutive interviews say DIFFERENT things, you have a polarized topic — investigate why.

5-8 interviews is typically enough for one segment. Less = anecdotal. More = diminishing returns until you change segments.

## After the interview

Within 24 hours:

- Write up key quotes (verbatim)
- Note your in-the-moment hypotheses
- Code themes (positive / negative / surprising)

Don't wait a week. Memory degrades fast.
